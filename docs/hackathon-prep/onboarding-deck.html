Why we're here

A pile of dumped mattresses, broken furniture and bin bags has been blocking the pavement on Vallance Road for three days.

It's attracting rats. Rebecca and her daughter have to step into the road to walk past.

She's reported it three times. Once via FixMyStreet. Once on the Tower Hamlets website. Once by emailing her local councillor.

She has heard nothing back from anyone.

This isn't a hypothetical user. We have FixMyStreet data showing this exact pattern: the same street, reported repeatedly, ignored for months. Our database now holds 6,000+ examples of Rebeccas across 24 London boroughs.

The protagonist matters more than the technology.Toronto's winners knew this.

05 / 13 The wedge

The pain isn't
the wait.
It's the silence.

Rory Sutherland, on why Uber was a step-change in user experience: "It didn't make the taxi faster. It told you when the taxi was arriving."

Same principle, applied to civic complaints. Rebecca can wait 28 days for that mattress to be cleared. What she can't tolerate is silence — not knowing if it'll be tomorrow, next month, or never.

Every Toronto winner this year — Belong, CityFlow — won by reducing uncertainty, not by being faster.

If we build only one thing perfectly, build this.The transparency layer.
06 / 13 Our pitch in one sentence

Every Londoner just got a £200/hour ombudsman.

It takes one photo and one voice note. The agent classifies the issue, scores its severity against cited London data, files it directly to the council's CRM, and doesn't stop chasing until it's fixed — by SMS, by phone call to the council, and ultimately by drafted letter to the Local Government Ombudsman and to the resident's MP.

No clicks. No 146-option dropdowns. No silence. Photos never leave the user's device. All inference runs locally on the NVIDIA hardware.

This is the line we lead every conversation with.Learn it cold.
07 / 13 How it works

Five agents.
One protagonist.

Each agent does one thing well, hands off to the next. The user only sees the start and the end.

01 · INTAKE

See the issue

Photo + voice arrive. Vision model classifies the issue. STT extracts the user's description. GPS lifted from EXIF.

Qwen2.5-VL · Scribe
02 · SEVERITY

Score the impact

RAG over London open data: TfL collision history, school proximity, footfall, census density. Returns a cited severity score.

Llama 3.3 70B · RAG
03 · ROUTE

Find the owner

Maps borough + category to the correct council department + submission channel. Open311 endpoint, web form, or email.

Tool calling
04 · SUBMIT

File the report

Posts directly to Camden's Open311 API. No FixMyStreet middleman. The report lands inside the council CRM.

Open311 · POST
05 · ESCALATE

Don't shut up

SLA breached → agent calls the council. Still ignored → drafts letter to LGO and MP. The phone call is the killer demo.

Killer demo moment #1

When the council ignores the SLA, the agent picks up the phone.

Two weeks pass. Camden hasn't acknowledged Rebecca's report. The agent doesn't email. It doesn't tweet. It calls Camden's customer service line, references the ticket number, asks for an update, logs the response.

It's polite. It says it's an automated call on behalf of a resident. It doesn't pretend to be human. It just refuses to disappear.

Public accountability, fairly measured.

Councils that respond fast and prioritise the right issues rise to the top. Councils that ignore residents — even with valid excuses — fall. Publicly. Updated daily.

10 / 13 Reading the room

Toronto already happened.
Here's what they rewarded.

NVIDIA ran the same hackathon in Toronto two weeks ago. 37 projects, six of them civic-complaint clones. None of those six won. The winners shared three patterns. We have all three.

Pattern 01

Pick a named protagonist.

Belong won Public Services by being for one specific person: a caregiver of someone with dementia. Not "users." Not "society."

We have Rebecca. And every other Londoner exhausted by reporting the same pothole twelve times.

Pattern 02

Replace something expensive.

CityFlow won Economic Systems by giving small shops a Bloomberg Terminal ($24k/yr) for free. Belong replaced live-in care ($60k+/yr).

We replace a £200/hour ombudsman — and the case worker, and the private investigator who chases the council.

Pattern 03

Privacy as the headline.

Both winners led with "your data never leaves the device." Not as a footnote — as the elevator pitch.

Rebecca's photo never uploads. Her address never goes to the cloud. Only the report leaves — and only to the one council that needs it.

We are not gambling. We are reading the rubric.Toronto told us what to build.
