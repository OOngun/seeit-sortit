## The origin story (60 seconds, internalise this)

**1822.** Charles Babbage — Londoner, mathematician, holder of Newton's chair at Cambridge — convinced the British Treasury to fund the world's first programmable computer: the **Difference Engine**. They gave him £17,000 — roughly £2.4M in today's money. The first government-funded computing project in history.

**1837.** While the Difference Engine was still unfinished, he designed something more ambitious: the **Analytical Engine**. It had everything a modern computer needs. CPU. Memory. Conditional branching. Loops. **Full Von Neumann architecture, a hundred years before John von Neumann was born.**

**1843.** Ada Lovelace, his collaborator, published the *Notes* — describing how the Analytical Engine could be programmed to compute Bernoulli numbers. **The world's first algorithm. Written by a woman. Before computer science was a field.**

**1864.** Babbage, now in his seventies and bitter, published *Observations on Street Nuisances* — a furious pamphlet about how London's public realm was decaying and the authorities did nothing about it. He literally fought to fix the city.

**1871.** He died. Neither machine had ever been finished. The Treasury had taken his money and his life's work and given him nothing.

**1991.** The Science Museum in South Kensington completed a working replica of the Difference Engine #2, built to his original specifications. **It worked perfectly.** It is on display today, twenty minutes from where we are presenting.

**2026.** We finish the job. Not by computing the future — by computing accountability for the present.

## Visual identity

**Aesthetic.** Brass, cogs, mechanical engineering schematics. Monochrome. Looks like a Royal Society engraving, not an AI startup pitch deck.

**Avoid.** NVIDIA green for our own brand (use it only where we're explicitly thanking NVIDIA — don't bleed sponsor colours into our identity). Neural network diagrams. Generic AI clichés. Three.js spinning objects.

## Talking points for judges (the "why Babbage?" question)

If a judge asks the natural question — *"so, why Babbage?"* — here's the 15-second answer:

> *"Because his unfinished work IS the project. Babbage built computing to fix a real problem — accurate mathematical tables that were killing sailors. Then he spent decades fighting Treasury bureaucracy. Then he wrote a pamphlet about London's public realm decay. We're doing all three of those things — better tools, urgent stakes, no choice but to ship. He died waiting for the government to deliver. We're not waiting."*
